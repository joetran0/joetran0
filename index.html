About Me

Bio

I am a Ph.D. student in Applied Mathematics at York University. My research interests lie at the intersection of mathematical epidemiology, dynamical systems, and functional analysis, with a particular focus on threshold phenomena such as the basic reproduction number and related spectral criteria in structured models.

My work involves the analysis of compartmental and structured epidemic models (including SEIR-type and within-host systems), where I study existence and stability of equilibria, bifurcation behaviour, and the role of parameters in disease invasion and persistence. I am especially interested in operator-theoretic and functional-analytic approaches that provide rigorous foundations for threshold dynamics in both finite- and infinite-dimensional setting.

In addition to theoretical analysis, I engage in numerical simulation and computational modeling to complement analytical results and explore biologically relevant scenarios. I am also passionate about teaching and mathematical communication, and I enjoy developing clear, structure materials that help students build intuition and confidence in mathematics.

Research Interests

  • Threshold dynamics, invasion criteria, and reproduction numbers
  • Compartmental and structured epidemic models (ODE, PDE, integro-differential)
  • Stability, bifurcation, and parameter sensitivity
  • Numerical simulation and model calibration

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is grounded in clarity, structure, and conceptual understanding. I believe that mathematics should be presented as a coherent system of ideas rather than a collection of formulas, and my goal as an instructor is to help students see the underlying structure that connects definitions, theorems, and applications.

I emphasize precise mathematical language and logical reasoning, while remaining attentive to the challenges students face when encountering abstract concepts for the first time. I strive to break complex ideas into accessible components, using carefully chosen examples and visual intuition to bridge the gap between formal theory and student understanding. In tutorials and office hours, I encourage students to articulate their reasoning, ask questions freely, and engage actively with the material.

I also believe that learning mathematics requires practice paired with feedback. I design problem sets and discussions that progress from foundational skills to deeper conceptual questions, allowing students to build confidence while developing mathematical maturity. Mistakes are treated as a natural and valuable part of the learning process, and I work to create a supportive environment where students feel comfortable exploring ideas and refining their understanding.

Ultimately, my aim is not only to help students succeed in a particular course, but also to equip them with problem-solving skills, critical thinking habits, and a lasting appreciation for the role of mathematics in science and beyond.
